# Better Coffee at Home

**Café-quality brewing, no espresso machine required**

A field guide to the four variables that decide every cup

---

<!-- _class: prose-with-tldr -->

## It's a brewing problem, not a bean problem

> **TL;DR:** Control four variables (grind, water temperature, ratio, and time) and ordinary beans make a genuinely good cup.

A brewed cup is about 98.7% water and roughly 1.3% dissolved coffee. The whole game is controlling *how much* coffee you extract and *how evenly*. Get the four levers right and a cheap bag of beans tastes great; get them wrong and the most expensive beans in the world taste flat, sour, or bitter. None of it needs barista training: just a scale, a decent grinder, and a little attention.

## Water matters as much as beans

<div class="stat">98.7%</div>

Of a brewed cup is plain water, so filtering it is the cheapest upgrade most people never make.

<!-- _class: prose -->

## Brewing is extraction: timing is everything

Hot water pulls flavor out of coffee in a fixed order: bright acids first, balanced sweetness next, harsh bitterness last. Stop too early and the cup is sour and thin. Run too long and it turns bitter and hollow.

Every variable is a lever on that timing: finer grind, hotter water, and longer contact all extract faster. A great cup simply sits in the sweet spot in the middle.

---

## You need less gear than you think

- A kitchen scale that reads in grams
- A burr grinder (not a blade grinder)
- A gooseneck or any controllable kettle
- Fresh, whole beans
- A pour-over cone or a French press
- Filtered water

## Part 3 · Brew it

---

<!-- _class: process-flow -->

## The pour-over, step by step

1. Heat water to 96°C, just off the boil
2. Rinse the filter and warm the cup
3. Add 15 g of ground coffee, level it
4. Bloom: pour 45 g, wait 30 seconds
5. Pour to 250 g in slow, steady circles
6. Let it draw down, then serve

---

<!-- _class: code-showcase -->

## A recipe worth keeping

```
Pour-over: single cup
──────────────────────────────
Coffee   15 g, medium-fine grind
Water    250 g, 96°C, filtered
Ratio    1 : 16.7

0:00   bloom: pour 45 g, wait 30 s
0:30   pour in circles to 150 g
1:15   pour to 250 g total
2:30   drawdown complete, serve
```

Tape it inside a cupboard. Too sour? Grind finer. Too bitter? Grind coarser.

| AeroPress     | Medium-fine  | 1:14    | 1.5 min   | Medium  |
| Espresso      | Fine         | 1:2     | 25–30 sec | Intense |
| Cold brew     | Coarse       | 1:8     | 12–18 hr  | Smooth  |

## Nine ways to ruin a cup

1. Stale, long-opened beans
2. Grinding too fine or too coarse
3. Never weighing coffee or water
4. Water straight off a rolling boil
5. Unfiltered or over-softened water
6. Pre-ground coffee gone flat
7. Pouring too fast, channeling the water
8. Skipping the 30-second bloom
9. Oily residue left on the gear

## The bottom line

You don't need a three-thousand-dollar machine to drink excellent coffee. A scale, a burr grinder, filtered water, and attention to ratio and grind will transform almost anyone's cup tomorrow morning. Start with the recipe, change one variable at a time, and taste as you go.
